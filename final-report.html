Section 1: Introduction

Background of the Data

This data set contains weekly retail prices for six fuel types: Regular Unleaded Gasoline, Mid‑Grade Gasoline, Premium Gasoline, Diesel, Auto Propane, and Compressed Natural Gas across 10 markets in Ontario. Prices are expressed in Canadian cents per litre, with compressed natural gas measured in cents per gasoline-equivalent litre. The data set spans from January 3, 1990 to March 16, 2026 and is updated weekly with the latest retail fuel price data published by the Government of Ontario.

Research Question

How does the price differ between cities in Ontario?

Section 2: Data Description

The data set contains 11,252 weeks of data from 1990 to March 16, 2026, covering six fuel types. Each row represents a weekly average of fuel prices, so daily fluctuations are not captured. It also includes three regional average columns (Ontario, Northern Ontario, Southern Ontario), which do not correspond to the simple average of the included cities, indicating they were sourced externally.

2. Handling of Zero Values

Values recorded as 0 in the city-level columns are interpreted as missing data, since these 0s do not represent actual prices. To prevent these zeros from affecting computations and interpretation of the data, all 0 values are converted to NA prior to analysis.

3. Conversion of the Date Column

The Date column, originally recorded as a character string, was converted to a Date type to ensure proper chronological ordering and accurate calculations. This allows for a better interpretation of the data, correct filtering, aggregation, and plotting over time, and ensures that weekly comparisons between cities are reliable.

4. Filtering Dates

After looking at the data, we notice that there is too much missing data before the year 2021, so we restrict the scope to data from January 1, 2021 and onwards. This ensures the analysis focuses on recent market trends rather than historical outliers.

5. Categorization of the Data Set

As mentioned above, the data set includes six fuel types. To enable clearer and more accurate comparisons between cities, the data will be analyzed separately for each fuel type.

To ensure our analysis provides a comprehensive view of the transportation sector, we will focus our statistical inference on four distinct fuel categories: Regular Unleaded Gasoline (representing the majority of consumer vehicles), Diesel (representing commercial transit), as well as Auto Propane (representing alternative fuels).

Section 5: Statistical Inference

To answer our research question on how prices differ across cities, we will compare the Northern Ontario Average price to the Southern Ontario Average price. To ensure our analysis provides a comprehensive view of the transportation sector, we will focus our statistical inference on four distinct fuel categories: Regular Unleaded Gasoline (representing the majority of consumer vehicles), Diesel (representing commercial transit), as well as Auto Propane (representing alternative fuels). Additionally, we have restricted our analysis to recent data (2021–2026) to ensure our findings reflect the current economic landscape and provide the most relevant insights. Because prices are recorded on the exact same weeks, we will analyze the paired differences (Northern Average - Southern Average).

5.1 Confidence Interval

We estimate difference in gas prices (Northern Average - Southern Average) across the available weeks between 2021-2026 for Regular Unleaded Gasoline, Diesel, .

Based on data from the last five years, we are 95% confident that the true average difference in fuel prices between Northern and Southern Ontario falls within the following intervals (in cents per litre):

5.2 Hypothesis Testing

To rigorously answer our research question regarding regional price differences, we conducted a paired two-sample t-test for each of our three fuel types. We set our significance level at \(\alpha = 0.05\).

Hypotheses: Let \(\mu_d\) represent the true mean difference in weekly fuel prices between Northern and Southern Ontario (\(\mu_{North} - \mu_{South}\)).

  • Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)): \(\mu_d = 0\). There is no true mean difference in fuel prices between Northern and Southern Ontario. (Any observed difference is due to random market fluctuations).
  • Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_1\)): \(\mu_d \neq 0\). There is a true, statistically significant mean difference in fuel prices between Northern and Southern Ontario.

Results:

Fuel Type p-value
Regular Unleaded Gasoline 1.8073e-11
Diesel < 2.22e-16

Conclusion:
As shown in the table above, the calculated p-values for all four fuel types—Regular Unleaded Gasoline, Diesel, Auto Propane, and Compressed Natural Gas—are strictly less than our significance level of 0.05. Because of this, the price differences for every fuel category are statistically significant, and we confidently reject the null hypothesis (\(H_0\)) in all cases.

Section 6: Bootstrapping

While the paired t-tests in Section 5 provided strong evidence of a regional price divide, parametric tests rely on the assumption that the sample means are normally distributed. Real-world commodities like fuel are subject to sudden supply shocks and extreme price volatility, which can introduce outlines into the data.

To ensure our findings are robust and not skewed by these potential outliers, we utilize a non-parametric approach: Bootstrapping. By resampling our modern (2021-2026) paired regional price differences 1,000 times with replacement, we can generate an empirical distribution of the mean difference without relying on assumptions of normality.

6.2 Visualization of Bootstarpped Means

To better conceptualize this process, we can visualize the 1,000 resampled mean differences for Regular Unleaded Gasoline. The red dashed lines represent the boundaries of our 95% Bootstrap Confidence Interval.

6.3 Interpretation

As demonstrated in the comparison table, our non-parametric Bootstrap confidence intervals are nearly identical to the classical parametric intervals calculated in Section 5. Furthermore, the visualized distributions of the resampled means for all three fuel types exhibit clear, bell-shaped curves that sit entirely above zero.

Section 7: Regression Analysis

In order to better understand the fuel price difference between the cities, we fit a regression model with time and city as predictors. From the weekly fuel price plot in Section 4, we can clearly see the correlation between time and fuel price is non-linear. Thus, we fit a polynomial regression model.

To prevent overfitting, we build the full model of maximum degree 5, and use cross-validation to determine the best degree model. To make sure the model perform well throughout this time period, we use 5-fold cross-validation.

### Filters the dataset for recent years (2021+) and runs paired t-tests to 
### calculate the average regional price difference, confidence intervals, and 
### statistical significance for gasoline, diesel, and auto propane.

# REGULAR GASOLINE
regional_compare_gas = regular_gas_data %>%
  filter(Date >= as.Date("2021-01-01")) %>%
  select(Date, Northern_Ontario_Average, Southern_Ontario_Average) %>%
  drop_na() %>%
  mutate(Reg_Diff = Northern_Ontario_Average - Southern_Ontario_Average)

                    regional_compare_gas$Southern_Ontario_Average,
                    paired = TRUE)
mean_diff_gas = t_test_gas$estimate
ci_lower_gas = t_test_gas$conf.int[1]

Section 6: Bootstrapping

### Mimics the concept of repeated sampling by using the bootstrap method to 
### generate distributions and 95% confidence intervals for regional 
### price differences across the three fuel types.

set.seed(57)
B = 1000      # Number of bootstrap iterations

get_boot_ci = function(df) {
  
  boot_function = function() {
    boot_data = df %>% slice_sample(n = nrow(df), replace = TRUE)
    boot_mean = boot_data %>% summarize(cal.s = mean(Reg_Diff, na.rm = TRUE))
    return(boot_mean[1,1])
  }
  
  # Replicate the process B times
  boot_means = replicate(B, boot_function())
  
  # 2.5% and 97.5% percentiles for the 95% CI
  ci = quantile(boot_means, probs = c(0.025, 0.975))
  return(c(lower = ci[1], upper = ci[2]))
}

# Section 5

boot_diesel = get_boot_ci(regional_compare_diesel)

diesel_mse = vector()
propane_mse = vector()

for(d in 1:5) {
  mse_fold = vector() 

  for(i in 1:5) {
    
    train_set = regular %>% filter(group_ind != i)

    m_train = lm(Price ~ City + poly(Time_Index, d), data = train_set)
